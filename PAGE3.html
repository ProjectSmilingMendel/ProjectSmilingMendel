Reasoning to transform Bacillus spp:
Some Bacillus spp., like other PGPR, can help plants grow by fixing nitrogen, dissolving phosphorus, making phytohormones, or reducing the effects of some abiotic stress factors. This group has species that are very good at stopping plant diseases. It explains how they make various enzymes, proteins, and small bioactive secondary metabolites (BSMs). Since it is clear that these BSMs help control pathogens, we stress how important it is to understand the ecological factors that affect their generation. In their typical environment, these bacteria may be very competitive. They live in groups on plant tissues and can take over the roots of both monocot and dicot species. Roots that can be colonized help make antibacterial compounds and plant resistance-inducing chemicals. This metabolite release makes it possible to fight a wide range of bacteria, viruses, and fungi that cause disease. These pathogens have different life cycles and ways of being harmful (biotrophic, hemibiotrophic, or necrotrophic). Biocontrol with Bacillus spp. has been shown to work in greenhouses, fields, and after food has been picked to treat diseases. A single strain can stop several harmful germs. It has also been shown that some Bacillus strains fight against oomycetes like Pythium aphanidermatum and Phytophthora infestans.
These bacteria are aerobic and proliferate at a relatively higher rate. They only need a little food to grow and can grow on various food sources. Most of the time, they make a variety of enzymes (cellulases, amylases, and proteases) that break down different substances like lignocellulose, starch, proteins, hydrocarbons, and biofuels. This means they can be grown in low-cost mediums, like plant raw materials in industrial bioreactors or solid-state fermentation, where they can be fermented. Bacillus species are primarily used as biocontrol agents because they can make various chemically different chemicals.
Comparative genomics has shown that plant-associated clades of the B. subtilis group are especially rich in biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) that code for bioactive secondary metabolites (BSMs). Up to 12 percent of the genome is used to make these chemicals. Some BGCs are found in many B. subtilis group species, while others seem more species-specific. Bacteriocins, like plantazolicin, subtilin, sericin, miracidia, amylolytic, subtilisin, and amylocyclicin, are made by B. subtilis and similar species. Chemotaxis helps roots find new homes: motility and the creation of biofilms.
